A Visual Story of Mortality in Malaysia

In Malaysia, the state and demographic-specific uneven patterns in mortality can be observed within the years between 2013 and 2023. The west coast states tend to have a higher death rate and some of the ethnic or gender groups become disproportionately affected. Chronic problems such as pneumonia and ischaemic heart diseases were the top threats when it comes to looking at what is causing these deaths, as of 2023. However, by moving back 2000-2021 we find a lower-prominence crisis, not rising suicide rates, but oscillating ones, particularly in males, reminding us that public health does not just concern the physical but the mental strength as well.

1. Where Are We Dying? Mapping Death Rates Across Malaysia

Our journey begins with a geographic overview. This map visualizes the Crude Death Rate (CDR) - the number of deaths per 1,000 people
for each of the following Malaysian states. The intensity of the red color directly reflects the death rate: darker shades indicate higher mortality,
while lighter shades represent lower rates. This allows for a quick visual assessment of regional health disparities.

Use the 'Select Year' dropdown (covering 2013-2023) to explore how patterns have evolved over the years. Data suggests states like Perlis
and Perak frequently exhibit consistently high death rates (often above 7.0 or 8.0). This trend could be linked to several factors, including
older population demographics in these states compared to the national average, potentially leading to a higher natural incidence of age-related
illnesses. Variations in healthcare access and infrastructure, particularly in more rural areas within these states, might also play a role.

Conversely, the federal territory of Putrajaya consistently shows the lowest rates (typically below 2.5), likely influenced by its status as an
administrative center with a predominantly younger, working-age population and possibly better access to centralized health facilities.
Selangor, being highly urbanized with generally good healthcare access, also maintains
lower rates despite its large population, though spikes can occur, note the significant increase across many states during 2021
(e.g., Perak peaked at 8.5, Selangor jumped to 6.0), reflecting the severe impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. The map automatically highlights
the states with the absolute highest (dark border) and lowest (blue border) CDR for the selected year.

2. Who Is Most Affected? Deaths by Ethnicity and Gender (2013-2023)

This map shows who is dying by visualizing the absolute number of deaths per state. It covers 2013-2023, broken down by ethnicity and gender.
The size of the symbol represents the total death count. This highlights areas with the largest overall mortality burden.

The Bumiputera Malay group consistently shows the highest absolute death totals. This aligns directly with them being the largest national demographic.
Chinese residents often show the second-highest counts. This group typically has an older demographic profile, increasing mortality risk. The Other
Bumiputera group generally records the lowest absolute numbers on the West Coast, reflecting their smaller population base. The overall male death
count is also consistently higher than female counts, suggesting higher occupational and behavioral risks. Putrajaya and Labuan always show the
lowest absolute numbers due to their small populations.

  • Year: See how total deaths change. Populous states (Selangor, Johor) show large symbols. Note the larger symbols across states in 2021.
  • Ethnicity (Color): Filter by Bumiputera Malay, Other Bumiputera, Chinese, Indian.
  • Gender (Shape): Compare males (▲), females (■), or view the combined total (●).

3. What Is Killing Us? Leading Causes in 2023

After exploring 'where' and 'who', this chart identifies what were the principal causes of death in Malaysia for 2023. It ranks the leading causes based on the total number of recorded deaths.

Pneumonia (🫁) clearly stands out as the predominant cause (with 18,181 deaths). Ischaemic Heart Diseases (🫀) follows as the second leading cause (with 18,121 deaths). Other major contributors include Cerebrovascular Diseases (🧠) and various forms of Cancer (💀). This provides a snapshot of the primary disease burdens.

4. The Quiet Crisis: Suicide Trends (2000-2021)

Shifting focus to mental health, this chart tracks the suicide rate (per 100,000 population) over two decades, comparing trends between males (blue line) and females (pink line). Data extends to 2021, reflecting reporting lags and capturing the COVID-19 pandemic impact on mental health.

This long-term view reveals a stark gender disparity. Female rates remain consistently low (generally below 2.5 per 100,000), whereas male rates are substantially higher and more volatile, peaking near 9.0 per 100,000 in 2021 which is three times more than the female. This highlights a significant public health concern, particularly for men.
